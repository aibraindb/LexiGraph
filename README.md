# Document Intelligence MVP (Bank)
Document Structures and Key Fields

Real-world financial and legal documents come in various forms, each with characteristic structures and fields. Here we examine five types – Guarantee Agreements, Delivery & Acceptance Certificates, Invoices, Loan Agreements, and Lease Agreements – using the provided examples as archetypes. These documents differ in layout: some are form-like with tables and labeled fields (e.g. invoices), while others are prose contracts with clauses (e.g. loan/lease agreements). Below we describe the typical layout, key fields, and variations for each type, noting how information like party names, addresses, terms, and signatures are presented.


Guarantee Agreements (Continuing Guaranty)

Example Guarantee Agreement (Continuing Guaranty) illustrating typical structure and fields. A Guarantee Agreement (often titled “Continuing Guaranty”) is usually a one to two-page legal document in which a Guarantor promises to fulfill a borrower’s obligations to a lender if the borrower fails to do so. Key structural elements include:
	•	Title and Date: The document often begins with a prominent title (“Continuing Guaranty”) and an effective date. For example, the provided Wells Fargo guaranty is labeled “Continuing Guaranty” with a date line (e.g. November 2, 2023).
	•	Parties and Addresses: It explicitly identifies the Creditor/Lender (e.g. a bank) and the Customer/Debtor whose obligations are being guaranteed, often with names and addresses. In our example, the “Name and Address of Customer” is listed (e.g. HOOD WELDING AND FABRICATION LLC, 3017 Country Dancer Ave, N. Las Vegas, NV…). The Guarantor (the party signing the guaranty) is also named; sometimes the guarantor’s address appears or is filled in a blank. The guaranty may be addressed “to [Lender]” indicating in whose favor it is made.
	•	Recitals/Definitions: Many guarantees start with a brief introduction or whereas clauses defining terms. For instance, they may define “Customer”, “Guarantor”, and reference the underlying credit or lease agreement being guaranteed.
	•	Obligation Clause: The core content is a clause where the Guarantor “unconditionally guarantees” payment and performance of the Customer’s obligations. This section often spans multiple paragraphs covering the scope (e.g. all present and future obligations), continuing nature (not revoked until certain conditions), and possibly waivers of notices or defenses.
	•	Terms and Conditions: The guaranty includes standard legal provisions – waivers of requirement to exhaust remedies on the borrower first, consent to extensions or modifications, subrogation terms, etc. These may be in numbered paragraphs or in a block of text.
	•	Signatures: A signature block for the Guarantor is typically at the bottom. It will have a line for the Guarantor’s signature (and name/title if a company). In personal guarantees, often an individual signs (sometimes with a notary acknowledgment if required). In our example image, the guaranty appears to have been electronically signed (noted by a DocuSign banner). The presence of “DocuSign Envelope ID” and “Authoritative Copy held at … docusign.net” indicates a digitally executed document, but a paper guaranty would have a physical signature line.

Variations: Guarantee agreements can be standalone (as here) or embedded as clauses in larger contracts. A Continuing Guaranty like this remains in force until revoked and covers multiple transactions. Some guaranties are limited (for a single loan or a fixed amount) – those would explicitly state the limit. The layout is usually a formal letter or short contract format, often on the lender’s letterhead or with a reference to the related loan/lease contract number. Key fields like parties’ names, addresses, dates, and the signature section are present in all cases, though their placement can vary (some forms have a header section listing the parties and contract references). Overall, the guarantee’s structure is relatively straightforward: identify parties, state the guarantee obligation, and include standard legal protections for the lender.

Delivery and Acceptance Certificates

Example Delivery & Acceptance Certificate confirming equipment delivery and lessee’s acceptance. A Delivery and Acceptance Certificate (often abbreviated D&A) is usually a one-page form that a customer/lessee signs to acknowledge they have received and accepted equipment under an associated contract (like a lease or financing agreement). Its layout is form-like and concise:
	•	Header and Reference Info: The title “Delivery and Acceptance Certificate” is prominent at the top. Often the certificate references the related contract. For example, the provided certificate lists an Account or Lease Number and date (e.g. “Account Number 301-0000280-003 dated as of December 11, 2023” for a Wells Fargo equipment finance program). It also shows the financing provider’s name/logo (Wells Fargo and a mention of the equipment supplier program, e.g. Trimble). Typically, the top section includes Name and Address of Customer (e.g. TRANS FLORIDA DEVELOPMENT CORP, address in Miami, FL in our example) and may include equipment identifiers or schedule IDs that this certificate covers. It’s common to see “TO:” addressing the finance company or lessor, and “RE:” referencing the master agreement or schedule number ￼.
	•	Acceptance Statement: The core text is a set of statements that the customer (lessee or borrower) certifies. For instance, the certificate will state that all the equipment described has been delivered and installed, inspected, and is irrevocably accepted as of a certain date ￼. It may include sub-points like: (a) equipment is delivered and in good working order, (b) the customer has inspected and tested it, (c) the customer accepts the equipment for all purposes of the agreement. In the example, the text confirms delivery/installation, that the customer has inspected and deems it acceptable, and directs the finance company to pay the vendor. It also usually asserts that the lessee is not in default of the main agreement and that representations remain true.
	•	No Warranties Clause: Many D&A certificates include a disclaimer that the lessor or lender makes no warranties about the equipment. For example, they often explicitly acknowledge that the equipment is accepted “AS IS” and that any issues or warranties are to be addressed with the vendor/manufacturer, not the finance company ￼. This protects the lessor by documenting the lessee’s acceptance and awareness that they cannot withhold payment due to equipment issues ￼.
	•	Signature Section: At the bottom, there is a signature block for the Customer/Lessee’s authorized signer. This includes a signature line, the signatory’s printed name and title, and the date of signature. In our example, a representative (e.g. a VP like Javier Melo, VP) signed and dated (12/12/2023) on behalf of the customer company. The certificate may preface the signature with a label like “Customer: [Company Name]” followed by the signature. Sometimes the lessor counter-signs, but typically the key purpose is the lessee’s signed acknowledgment.
	•	Formatting: Most of these certificates are formatted as a short form or letter. They might have fillable blanks for key info (dates, contract numbers, equipment descriptors). Some include a small table or list of equipment (especially if multiple items – e.g. listing serial numbers or descriptions). Others, like the example, keep equipment details in the referenced schedule and just generically refer to “all Equipment” under that schedule.

Variations: The structure is fairly standard across finance and lease companies. A D&A certificate for, say, a vehicle lease or an aircraft might explicitly list the asset (e.g. VIN or aircraft tail number) in a table or in the text ￼. In equipment leasing, the D&A is usually required for funding: the lessor will not pay the vendor until this certificate is signed by the lessee confirming the goods are satisfactory ￼. Some forms include a warning not to sign until equipment is received (the example text explicitly says “DO NOT SIGN… UNTIL THE EQUIPMENT HAS ACTUALLY BEEN RECEIVED AND ACCEPTED” ￼). Overall, it’s a short document with a mix of fillable fields (names, dates, contract refs) and boilerplate acceptance text, always culminating in the lessee’s signature.

Invoices

Example Invoice with header fields and an itemized table. An Invoice is a commercial document issued by a seller to request payment for products or services provided to a buyer. Invoices have a structured layout that is often semi-standardized, typically including the seller’s branding and detailed line items. Key components of invoice layout include:
	•	Header Section: The top of an invoice contains the Seller’s information (company name, logo, address/contact) and the Invoice metadata. In the example, KELLY Office Solutions (logo at top-left) is the vendor. The invoice header shows fields like Invoice Number (e.g. 499295), Invoice Date (e.g. 12/27/2023), Account Number, and Balance Due. Common header fields also include a Purchase Order (P.O.) number (customer’s reference if applicable), and payment terms (e.g. “Due upon receipt” or Net 30 days). Indeed, our sample shows “Payment Terms: Due upon receipt” and a Payment Due Date (1/26/2024) in a summary box.
	•	Bill-To and Ship-To: Invoices clearly specify the Bill To (the customer who is paying) and often a Ship To (the delivery address, if different). In the example, Bill To is Wells Fargo Equipment Finance at their address in Des Moines, IA, while Ship To is St. Mark’s Church in Charlotte, NC (indicating Wells Fargo is financing the purchase for the end customer). These address blocks typically include the recipient name, company, address, and maybe contact person. They are often labeled and placed side by side or one above the other.
	•	Line Item Table: The core of an invoice is a table of line items detailing what is being billed. Each line usually has columns such as Item or SKU code, Description, Quantity, Unit Price, Discount, Line Amount, etc. In our example image, the table contains items like an equipment model (“IM C4510” copier with a serial number) and accessories (e.g. “Booklet Finisher SR3330”). Each line shows a quantity (possibly 1 each), a price, any discount (some items may be $0 if included), and an amount. The table often has a header row (highlighted in the image) with labels like “Description”, “Qty”, “Unit Price”, “Amount”.
	•	Subtotals and Totals: Below the item list, invoices sum up charges. Common fields: Subtotal, Tax (if applicable), Shipping (if applicable), and Total due. The example shows a Balance Due: $16,446.45 at top; likely detailed totals would be at bottom (the difference between the large equipment price and balance due could imply a down payment or credit was applied, though not visible, or multiple invoices). If taxes were applied, the invoice would list tax rates and amounts. In this case, it might be tax-exempt (since a church and financing).
	•	Footer and Terms: Invoices often include payment instructions or terms in the footer. For example, banking details (account number, bank name, etc) might be given if wire transfer is expected. They might also include a note like “Thank you for your business” or other terms (e.g. late payment interest or warranty of goods). In our example, we see a “Sales Person: Tom Skeels” noted, and likely on the actual full invoice there would be contact info or a remittance address at the bottom.
	•	Visual Layout: Invoices are typically very structured: seller info and invoice info at top, addresses next, then a clearly delineated table of charges, then totals. They often use boxes or bold labels to highlight key fields (like the invoice number and total due).

Variations: Invoice formats vary by company, but the essential fields are consistent. Some invoices include additional fields such as Customer ID, Terms (Net 30 etc.), Invoice Due Date, or Tax ID numbers of the vendor. If the invoice is part of a financing scenario (as our example, financed by Wells Fargo), it may list the financing company as the bill-to. In government or international invoices, you might see fields like Tax Registration Number, IBAN/Swift for payments, etc. Item tables can vary in columns; for services, you might see “Hours” and “Rate” instead of quantity and unit price. Despite cosmetic differences, any realistic invoice will contain identifiers (invoice number/date), parties (buyer/seller info), an itemized list of charges, and the final amount due. These fields provide a rich source of structured data for OCR systems (names, dates, monetary amounts, etc.), which are fairly standard across datasets.

Loan Agreements

Example Loan Agreement (Master Loan & Security Agreement) with multi-page clauses. Loan agreements are lengthy contracts that detail the terms of a loan between a lender and borrower. In equipment finance contexts, you often find a Master Loan Agreement with general terms, plus separate schedules for each loan. The structure of a loan agreement typically includes:
	•	Title and Parties: The document title indicates the type (e.g. “Master Loan and Security Agreement”). Early in the document, the parties are identified: the Lender (e.g. a bank or financing entity) and the Borrower/Customer (with legal name and address). In the example, the header shows Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (lender) and CC REAL ESTATE GROUP with an address in Corona, CA as the customer. Often a contract reference number and date are given (e.g. Master Agreement No. 420-0003769 dated June 7, 2024).
	•	Recitals/Intro: Some agreements start with “WHEREAS” clauses or an introductory sentence stating that the borrower desires to obtain financing and the lender agrees to lend subject to terms.
	•	Definitions Section: Loan agreements usually have a section defining key terms (e.g. “Collateral,” “Event of Default,” “Loan Documents,” etc.). These definitions can be listed at the beginning or in a schedule.
	•	Main Terms: The body is organized into sections or paragraphs covering all aspects of the loan. Common sections include: Loan Amount and Disbursement (how much is being lent, how funds are delivered), Interest Rate and Payment Terms (interest calculation, payment schedule, due dates), Security (if it’s a secured loan, a grant of security interest in collateral is described – e.g. equipment as collateral as indicated by “Security Agreement” in the title), Representations and Warranties of the borrower, Covenants (promises the borrower makes, like maintaining insurance on equipment, providing financial statements, etc.), Events of Default (what constitutes default and remedies available to lender), and Miscellaneous/General Provisions (governing law, assignment, notices, etc.).
	•	Schedules and Tables: If the agreement is a master agreement, specific loan details (equipment description, amounts, payment amortization) might not be in the main body but in Schedule or Promissory Note documents. However, some loan contracts include an amortization table or payment schedule as an exhibit. The example is labeled “Page 1 of 3”, suggesting further pages might list terms or possibly schedule of payments. Key financial figures (loan principal, interest rate, term length) would appear either on the first page or in a schedule. For instance, an equipment loan schedule might resemble an invoice/table listing each piece of equipment, cost, and payment amounts.
	•	Signatures: At the end of the loan agreement (typically the last page), there will be signature blocks for both the Borrower (authorized signatory of the borrowing company) and the Lender (a representative of the bank/finance company). The example image only shows page 1, but page 3 likely contains the signature lines (often labeled with each party’s name and title). If it’s a notarized or witnessed signing, there may be additional notarization blocks.
	•	Notices Addresses: Many contracts include a section listing the official addresses for each party for sending notices. In our archetype, the top of page 1 essentially provides the customer’s address and the lender’s address (Irving, TX for Wells Fargo’s division), which likely serve as notice addresses.

Variations: Loan agreements can vary widely in complexity. A simple equipment loan contract might only be a few pages with basic terms, whereas a syndicated corporate loan could be dozens of pages. The Master Loan & Security Agreement format, as in our example, is common for equipment financing: it allows multiple loan schedules under one master contract. In such cases, the master sets general terms and each schedule (or Loan Agreement Schedule) provides specifics for each batch of equipment financed. Layout-wise, these documents are dense text, often formatted in sections (sometimes numbered or lettered clauses). They usually lack the kind of tabular data seen in invoices, except possibly for schedules. Important data fields – like loan number, dates, borrower name, etc. – are typically concentrated on the cover or first page in a quasi-form layout (as we see with the preamble listing the customer name and agreement number). Subsequent pages are usually purely paragraph text. For OCR, loan agreements present the challenge of lots of legal verbiage but consistent identification of parties and key terms in predictable locations (top of first page, and signatures at the end).

Lease Agreements

Example Lease Agreement (Master Equipment Lease) showing definitions and clauses. Lease agreements (particularly equipment leases) are similar to loan agreements in structure, as they are also lengthy contracts, but they govern the leasing (renting) of equipment rather than an outright loan. A Master Lease Agreement sets up terms for leasing equipment under one contract with separate schedules for each set of equipment. Key structural elements:
	•	Title and Parties: The title will indicate it’s a lease (e.g. “Master Lease Agreement”). The parties are the Lessor (the finance company or equipment owner) and the Lessee (the customer using the equipment). In the example, HYG Financial Services, Inc. is the lessor and IAP Worldwide Services, Inc. is the lessee, with addresses listed. As with the loan, a master lease has an identifying number and date (e.g. Master Lease No. 400-0009192 dated Dec 15, 2023).
	•	Lease-Specific Terms: Lease agreements include many of the same components as loans (definitions, payment terms, default clauses) but with lease-specific concepts. Common sections: Lease Term and Rent – how long the lease is and payment amounts/frequency; Equipment – a description of the equipment being leased (often referenced in each schedule); Delivery and Acceptance – tying in the D&A certificate process (the lease often states that the lessee’s obligations commence upon signing a delivery and acceptance certificate confirming equipment is received ￼); Maintenance and Use – lessee’s responsibility to maintain equipment, allow inspections; Insurance – requiring lessee to insure the equipment; Taxes – clarifying sales or property tax responsibilities; Ownership – stating the lessor retains ownership (in a true lease) and tagging the equipment with ownership labels; End-of-term Options – such as purchase option, renewal, or return conditions; Warranty Disclaimer and “Hell or High Water” clause – lessee accepts equipment as-is and must pay rent regardless of issues ￼ ￼. Indeed, our example likely includes clauses where Lessee acknowledges no warranties by Lessor and the obligation to pay rent is unconditional.
	•	Security Interest and UCC filings: In a finance lease, there may be language that the lease is a “net lease” and that the lessor may file UCC-1 financing statements (to protect its interest in the equipment). The example being a master lease from a financial services company likely includes such provisions.
	•	Structure and Layout: The layout is contract text, often with numbered paragraphs or sections. The provided image shows a portion with definitions like “Equipment,” “Fair Market Value,” “Lease,” etc. enumerated at the start – a typical structure where Section 1 is definitions. Later sections would cover the items listed above (Term/Rent, obligations, etc.). These agreements can be several pages long, so they might not contain tables except perhaps a payment schedule or equipment list in an annex. Sometimes an Equipment Schedule (an exhibit or separate document) lists each item being leased, its cost, and the rent payment calculation – effectively a table with item description, serial number, cost, etc., and an associated rent payment amount. The master lease will refer to these schedules (e.g. “Equipment Schedule No. 1”) rather than listing gear in the main body ￼.
	•	Signatures: The signing page will have the lessor and lessee signature blocks. Often, each equipment schedule is also signed separately. In practice, the master lease may be signed once, and each schedule (with its own mini-agreement terms and acceptance) signed when equipment is added. The main agreement example likely had signatures for HYG Financial Services and IAP Worldwide at the end of the contract text.

Variations: Lease agreements vary if they are operating leases vs. finance leases, but most equipment lease contracts in financing contexts are “finance leases” (lessee is responsible for maintenance, taxes, etc., and often has an option to purchase). The presence of a “hell or high water” clause (unconditional payment obligation) and warranty disclaimers is standard ￼ ￼. The structure is similar to loan agreements, with heavy legal text. Some lease contracts, especially for vehicles or smaller equipment, might be partly in a form format (filling in equipment description, rent, etc. on the first page) and the rest in fine-print terms. Our example being a master lease likely has a cover page (with a short form listing customer, lessor, maybe a summary of lease term) followed by many pages of boilerplate. In any case, the key fields include party names, addresses, the lease number/date, equipment info (directly or via schedule), payment amounts or calculation method, and signatures. For OCR and analysis, detecting these fields often means locating the first-page summary and the final signature page among the dense text.

Datasets and Synthetic Document Generation

To train and evaluate an OCR+classification system on these document types, we need data. Unfortunately, fully public datasets containing guarantee agreements, loan/lease contracts, or delivery certificates are scarce due to their sensitive nature. However, we can leverage a combination of open datasets for analogous documents and synthetic document generation to create realistic examples:
	•	Open Datasets for Similar Documents: A widely used dataset is RVL-CDIP, which contains 400,000 scanned document images categorized into 16 classes ￼. Relevant classes include Invoices and Forms: for example, RVL-CDIP provides many invoice samples and other business forms that could resemble certificates or agreements. This dataset can supply real invoice images and some contract-like documents (though not labeled at fine granularity, “form” and “letter” classes may include contracts). Another dataset, FUNSD, is a smaller set of form documents with labeled field regions – useful for understanding form layouts, though those are more like questionnaires or administrative forms than contracts. There are also specialized datasets for invoices and receipts (e.g. SROIE for receipts, CORD for receipts, etc.), and while not exactly our documents, they contain itemized tables and can help train models to extract structured data from financial docs. For contracts, some research datasets like DocLayNet or Kleister have legal documents (for instance, Kleister-NDA has nondisclosure agreements). Additionally, the IIT-CDIP (which RVL-CDIP sampled) contains many scanned financial documents; with some effort, one can filter out sample loans or leases from it if labeled by keywords. We might also find sample contracts in public domains: for example, U.S. SEC EDGAR filings often include credit agreements, loan agreements, and guaranties as exhibits (usually in text PDF form). These can be scraped to provide raw text or converted to images for training (assuming no copyright issues for internal research).
	•	Synthetic Document Generation: To complement real data, generating synthetic documents is a powerful approach. We want synthetic guarantee agreements, acceptance certificates, invoices, loans, and leases that are as realistic as possible in structure and content:
	•	Templates: We can design document templates for each type. For instance, take a generic contract template for a guarantee or lease (there are templates available online or from law libraries) and replace specific details with fictitious ones. For the certificate and invoice, which are more form-like, we could create a form layout in a word processor or LaTeX that mirrors the examples (company logo placement, table structure, etc.).
	•	Faker Data: Use libraries like Faker in Python to generate realistic random entity details – e.g. company names, person names, addresses, dates, dollar amounts. This ensures our synthetic docs have plausible-sounding content. For example, generate random LLC names for customers, random banks for lenders, various addresses across cities, and realistic dates (perhaps spanning last few years).
	•	Populating Fields: For each template, populate the fields with the fake data. For an invoice, this means populating vendor and client details, random invoice numbers (maybe in different formats like INV-2025-0001), a set of line items (random product names or service descriptions with quantities and prices that sum to a total, plus random payment terms). For a delivery & acceptance certificate, generate random equipment descriptions or references, perhaps different lease contract numbers and dates, and a random signatory name/title. For loan/lease agreements, one can generate multi-page text. This is more challenging to do manually, so it might be beneficial to leverage a language model to produce filler legal text: for instance, prompt an LLM (Large Language Model) with an outline of a loan agreement and let it generate clauses (ensuring not to use any real data). Alternatively, use real contract text from public sources (like SEC filings of similar contracts) and just alter names/amounts.
	•	Maintaining Realism: Ensure the format of synthetic documents matches real ones. This includes using appropriate fonts (often business documents use Times New Roman or similar serif fonts at ~10-12pt), including logos or letterheads for authenticity (maybe use a placeholder image or a fake logo design for the vendor/lender company), and respecting margins and spacing conventions of formal documents. For contracts, maintain consistent section numbering and legal phrasing. For invoices, ensure the table has aligned columns and possibly gridlines. Small details like including a signature line (even if we just put a cursive-looking font to simulate a signature, or leave it blank) can add realism.
	•	Converting to Images: Since we want to simulate scanned documents, it’s effective to generate these documents as PDFs (e.g. using ReportLab or Microsoft Word to create the layout, then save as PDF) and then render them to image bitmaps. We can introduce noise or slight blur to mimic scanning. Another trick is to slightly rotate or skew the image or add a light “paper texture” background to make it look like a scan of a printed page rather than a crisp digital PDF. Tools like OpenCV or PIL can be used to add Gaussian noise, or we can print and literally scan/photograph a few for variety.
	•	Augmenting Variation: We should create multiple variants for each document type. For example, generate 10 different invoice layouts (varying placement of the bill-to section, maybe some with color logos, some with monochrome), so the model doesn’t overfit to one template. For contracts, use different wording or clause order (some guarantee forms might list certain waivers in different sequence). Also consider variations in length – e.g. one lease agreement might be 5 pages, another 8 pages – to teach the system that document type is not purely based on page count. Signature variations are useful too: some synthetic contracts could have a signature image pasted in, others just “/s/ John Doe” text, etc., to mimic real scenarios.
	•	Existing Synthetic Data Resources: We can note that organizations have tools for generating synthetic financial documents. For example, Goldman Sachs has described a synthetic data generator for contracts which models real data statistics to create fake yet realistic contracts ￼. While their scenario is more about generating JSON data for OTC derivatives, the idea of capturing polymorphic structure and then randomizing values is applicable. Also, AI services like Gretel or MostlyAI provide synthetic data generation for PII, which could be used to create fake personal and company names that look realistic. If coding from scratch is challenging, one might use a language model (like GPT-4 via API) with prompts to output, say, a short guaranty agreement given some parameters (party names, date, etc.). This can be done by few-shot prompting with examples of contract language. Just be cautious to verify the output – ensure it contains the fields we need (LLM might sometimes omit a section unless prompted specifically).

In summary, combining open data (for example, real invoice scans from RVL-CDIP or sample contracts from public domain) with synthetically generated documents will yield a robust dataset. Each synthetic document will include the relevant fields (names, addresses, dates, terms, tables, signatures) in context, providing training material for OCR text extraction and for learning the layout patterns of each doc type. By controlling the generation, we can balance classes and include edge cases (e.g. an invoice with multiple pages, a lease agreement with unusual formatting, etc.). All synthetic examples should be labeled by type (since we know which template we used) to supervise a classifier.

OCR Extraction and Preprocessing Best Practices

Extracting text and layout from scanned documents is a critical step. We recommend using PaddleOCR (an open-source OCR engine known for its accuracy on multilingual and multi-layout text) to get text content and bounding boxes for each text element. To maximize OCR performance, especially on scans that might be rotated or skewed, consider the following best practices:
	•	Preprocessing for Rotation and Skew: It’s important to deskew and correctly orient images before feeding to OCR. Even a 5-degree tilt can reduce OCR accuracy. A common approach is to perform skew angle detection by analyzing the text lines. For instance, one can apply a Hough transform on the detected lines or use projection profiles to estimate the dominant text angle. Once the skew angle is found (e.g. via the projection of connected components or using an FFT-based method), rotate the image to correct it. For rotation by 90 or 180 degrees (e.g. upside-down scans), PaddleOCR includes an angle classification model that can auto-detect and correct orientation. Ensure this classifier is enabled (in PaddleOCR’s Python API, there is usually a flag or a separate step for orientation – enabling it will rotate text lines to 0° or 180° as needed). If not using Paddle’s classifier, you could do a simple check: OCR a few words and detect if many characters are rotated or if the script is mostly vertical, then rotate image accordingly.
	•	Corner Detection and Perspective Correction: For photos of documents (common when using phone cameras), perspective distortion is a problem – the image may be taken at an angle, causing the document to appear trapezoidal. To handle this, you can apply corner detection and homography transform to obtain a flat, frontal view. Tools like OpenCV can find document contours: by converting to grayscale, applying edge detection (Canny) and finding the largest quadrilateral contour, you can detect the four corners of the paper. Once you have the corner coordinates, use cv2.getPerspectiveTransform and cv2.warpPerspective to rectify the document. This yields a top-down view as if scanned. There are also dedicated models like DeePaperScan which use a neural network to detect the 4 corner points of a document in an image. DeePaperScan was trained on smartphone photos of documents (from the SMARTDOC dataset) and can automatically give you the corner coordinates, which you then warp to flatten the page. By rectifying the image, you remove perspective skew, making OCR far more reliable (text lines become horizontal and properly scaled). If implementing from scratch is complex, note that many mobile scanner apps (and OpenCV tutorials) demonstrate this pipeline – detect edges, approximate contour, four-point warp. It’s a worthwhile preprocessing step when dealing with photographs or scans that are not perfectly aligned.
	•	Image Cleaning: After rotation and perspective fix, consider some image enhancements:
	•	Denoising and Binarization: Scans may have noise or yellowed backgrounds. Converting to a clean grayscale or binary image can help. However, modern OCR like PaddleOCR uses a neural network that can handle grayscale or color well, so binary thresholding isn’t always needed. If the scan is extremely noisy or has artifacts (e.g. scanner streaks), you might apply a filter. A gentle Gaussian blur can help remove salt-and-pepper noise (but too much blur will hurt character edges). Adaptive thresholding can turn a faded document into black-and-white, which can improve older Tesseract-like OCR, but PaddleOCR’s model might not require it. It’s good to experiment. In many cases, simply ensuring consistent lighting (maybe by doing an illumination correction) is beneficial.
	•	Resize for OCR model: Ensure the image is at a reasonable resolution for OCR. PaddleOCR’s text detector works on scaled images (it will resize large images down to a maximum size while preserving aspect ratio to fit the model’s expected input size). Extremely high DPI scans (e.g. 600 DPI) can be downsampled to speed up processing, but keep the text at least ~10-12 pixels high for clarity. Conversely, if the text is very small, you might upsample a bit or use a high-res mode.
	•	Color and Contrast: Increasing contrast or converting to pure grayscale can help if the document has color backgrounds or watermarks. Simple contrast stretching or use of OpenCV’s cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY) then cv2.equalizeHist() can enhance faint text.
	•	Using PaddleOCR effectively: PaddleOCR provides a pipeline: first it does text detection (finding text regions), then recognition on those regions, and it can output each text line (or word) with its bounding box (often a quadrilateral since it can handle rotated text lines). For our documents, text is mostly horizontal, so those quadrilaterals should be nearly rectangles. Make sure to use a language model appropriate for the content (PaddleOCR has English, multi-language, etc.). For our use case, an English model would suffice for the main text. We should parse PaddleOCR’s output to get both the text and coordinates.
	•	The output bounding boxes might be in image pixel coordinates. We can leave them as is, or convert to relative coordinates (e.g. normalized 0-1 or 0-1000 as needed for encoding later). It’s important to preserve the association of each text snippet with its position.
	•	If high accuracy is needed for critical fields (like total amounts on invoices), consider running a secondary pass or adjusting OCR settings. For example, if PaddleOCR misreads a stylized font, you could try Tesseract as a fallback on that region, or vice versa. But PaddleOCR is state-of-the-art and generally robust.
	•	Multi-Column or Reading Order: Documents like invoices are often multi-block (addresses on left, items on table, totals at bottom-right). OCR will return text in essentially random order (often left-to-right, top-to-bottom, but when columns exist, it might jump between columns). For downstream tasks, it’s useful to determine a proper reading order or group text by region. One approach is to cluster OCR bounding boxes spatially – e.g. use their coordinates to identify blocks corresponding to address vs table vs footer. This can be done by simple rules (x-coordinate thresholds or using the fact that tables have aligned columns). Another approach is to sort by y coordinate, then x, but in invoices that might intermix columns. A more advanced solution is to train a layout analysis model or use something like LayoutParser to classify layout regions (header, table, etc.). Depending on needs, this may or may not be necessary. If our goal is classification by document type, exact reading order is less critical. If we want information extraction (like “find the total amount”), then understanding layout is important.
	•	Validation: Always manually inspect some OCR outputs during development. Check that critical fields are correctly recognized (names, dollar amounts, dates). If some are consistently wrong (e.g. perhaps the OCR confuses “O” and “0” in certain fonts for invoice numbers), you might add a post-processing step or a custom pattern recognition (for instance, if expecting an invoice number format, use regex to correct obvious errors).

By following these practices – deskewing, correcting orientation, flattening perspective, and tuning the OCR pipeline – we can obtain a high-quality transcription of each document along with bounding box coordinates for each word or line. This forms the basis for the next stages: encoding the layout for NLP and performing classification or clustering.

Coordinate Encoding Schemes for NLP

Once we have OCR text and bounding boxes, we need to encode this information for downstream NLP tasks (like feeding into a model that classifies documents or extracts fields). There are a few common encoding schemes to represent text along with its layout coordinates:
	•	Bracketed Bounding Box Format: In this approach, we embed the coordinate info directly in the text sequence by enclosing each word (or line) in brackets with its box coordinates. For example, a word “Customer” at coordinates (x0=50,y0=100,x1=150,y1=120) might be represented as [Customer 50 100 150 120]. A full address line might become [123 Main 50 120 180 135][Street 185 120 240 135]... etc., each word with its own coordinates. This literal inline annotation makes the sequence quite long but self-contained. The advantage is that a text-based model (like a transformer or even an LLM) could potentially learn to interpret these numbers as positional clues. However, this format is verbose and treats coordinates as just more “tokens”, which might not be ideal for a model not specifically trained to handle position info as tokens. It could also exceed model token limits quickly if a document has thousands of tokens (since each actual word token is now accompanied by four numeric tokens). That said, it’s straightforward for debugging and for simpler downstream parsing. If using this, one might consider downsampling the coordinates (e.g. do not use full pixel values like 1243, 3241 – perhaps scale them down or round to reduce vocabulary of numbers).
	•	Compact Tuple Format: A variant of the above is a more compact notation like Label (x0 y0 x1 y1); for each element. Here we might combine words into a larger unit or label certain fields. For example, one could label the “Bill To” address block as a whole: BILL_TO_ADDR(50 200 300 350); to denote that a block spans those coords. Or each line: CustomerName(50 100 150 115);CustomerAddress(50 120 200 150);. This seems to imply using semantic labels (like “CustomerName”) and attaching coordinates. It could be useful if you already have a structured understanding of the document (e.g. from an earlier information extraction step that tags fields). If not, one could simply use the text itself as the label (like the word or line) similar to bracketed format but using parentheses and semicolon as separators. For instance, Customer (50 100 150 115); meaning the word “Customer” is at those coords. This removes the square brackets and puts coordinates in parentheses, which might be slightly easier for some parsers to handle. It’s mostly a stylistic difference from the bracket notation – both are human-readable. The compact tuple format might also be used to concatenate multi-word fields into one tuple, which can significantly reduce the length. For example, instead of tagging each word in an address, you tag the whole address line or block with one tuple. This sacrifices some granularity but makes the sequence shorter. Depending on the task, that may be acceptable or even preferable (if classifying document type, you don’t need every word separate; if doing field extraction, labeling whole fields might be more intuitive).
	•	Binned Coordinate Format (0-1000 scale): This is a technique used by models like LayoutLM. The idea is to normalize all coordinates to a fixed range – typically 0 to 1000 – regardless of the actual image pixel size. For each token (word piece), the bounding box coordinates (xmin, ymin, xmax, ymax) are scaled to [0,1000] and then discretized to integers. For example, if a document image is 2550 pixels wide, the token’s x coordinates might be scaled as x0_norm = floor((x0 / 2550)*1000). The purpose is to have a consistent coordinate range for the model’s embedding layers. In LayoutLM, these normalized coords are fed into learned position embeddings, rather than being appended to text. However, if we want to encode them in text form, we could still do something similar. For instance, instead of writing full pixel values, we could write coordinates on a 0-1000 scale. So a token at (x0=50, y0=100, x1=150, y1=120) on an image of width ~600 and height ~800 might become (83, 125, 250, 150) after normalization (just as an example). Rounding to integer and perhaps zero-pad to fixed width (three or four digits) could keep the format consistent. Another approach to binning is to discretize not to 1000 but to a smaller set of buckets (like define a grid and give coordinates in terms of grid cell indices). But 1000 has become a de-facto standard because it provides a fine resolution and fits in two bytes which some models use to pack into embeddings.
	•	Choosing a Scheme: If using a transformer-based model that explicitly supports layout features (like LayoutLM family, or LiLT, etc.), you wouldn’t embed coordinates in the text at all; you would pass them as separate features to the model’s forward pass. For example, with HuggingFace’s LayoutLM models, you supply for each token the normalized bounding box (x0,x1,y0,y1 in 0-1000) and the model has learned embeddings for those positions. This is the most robust way if such a model is available, because it leverages pre-training that expects those coordinates. However, if we are trying to do this with a generic NLP model or even a local LLM via prompting, we might need to stick to an inline text representation.
The bracketed or tuple formats are useful when you want to feed data into a GPT-style model via a prompt. For instance, you could prompt: “Here is an OCR of a document with each text and its bounding box: [Lease 100 50 180 70][Agreement 185 50 250 70]… Given this, classify the document type.” An LLM can read those, but a lot of coordinates might be noise to it unless cleverly prompted to use them (perhaps not needed just for classification). On the other hand, if doing clustering or classical ML on features, you might not use these textual encodings at all; instead, you might derive layout features like word appears in top 10% of page = likely a title, etc.
The binned coordinate embedding approach is recommended if using models like LayoutLM or if we roll our own model. By limiting to 1000, we ensure the coordinate values (which will become part of the vocabulary or embedding input) are bounded. This avoids having a giant vocabulary of every possible pixel. For example, instead of a number token like “1243” which might be rare, we’d have at most “1000” as the largest token for coordinate (or we just feed the numbers as numbers in a consistent width).
	•	Hybrid: We can also combine schemes. For example, one could produce a single sequence where each token is represented as token|x_bin|y_bin (a sort of markup) and then use a tokenizer that can split that into meaningful pieces. Or if using a network, one could concatenate numeric features to the token embedding. But keeping it simpler: for a CPU-friendly pipeline, it might be easier to use the text-with-coordinates approach for any advanced reasoning with a general model, or to use normalized numeric arrays if doing custom classification.

Recommendation: For downstream classification of document type, we might not actually need per-token coordinates – a bag-of-words or sequence of text could suffice since the content itself is a strong signal (e.g. words like “Invoice” or presence of a table of prices signal an Invoice, phrases like “Lessee” and “Master Lease” signal a Lease Agreement, etc.). However, if doing fine layout-based tasks or clustering by layout similarity, including coordinates is useful. A practical approach is to use normalized coordinates (0-1000) and either feed them into a specialized model or encode them in text in a minimal way. For example, one could compress each token’s coordinates to a short tag like <coord_83_125_250_150> or even just attach category like <header> if we pre-classify regions.

In summary, the bracketed format is human-readable and easy to generate, good for initial experiments or prompting. The tuple format can be used if we want to label certain fields and group coordinates (e.g. treat address block as one tuple rather than many tokens). The binned format is crucial if we plan to use neural models that expect numeric layout features – scaling to 1000 is a proven strategy. We should choose based on our next step: since we plan to cluster and classify documents, we might not need every token’s position, but rather global layout features or at least the positions of key phrases. One could, for instance, represent a document by the distribution of words in different vertical regions (like a histogram of words in top vs bottom half). That is another form of encoding layout that’s more abstract. But if using an all-in-one model (like LayoutLM or an LLM with markup), the above schemes are the go-to options.

Document Embedding, Clustering, and Classification

With OCR text (and possibly encoded layout), we can generate embeddings for each document and use those for clustering and classification by type. The goal is to group similar documents (so that, say, all invoices cluster together) and to train or infer document type labels from these embeddings. Here are steps and best practices for this stage, focusing on local (CPU-friendly) methods:
	1.	Tokenization & Text Embedding: We need to convert each document’s content into a numerical vector (embedding) that captures its semantics (and possibly layout). A straightforward way is to use a pre-trained language model to obtain a document-level embedding. Since we prefer a CPU-friendly approach, we might use a lighter model like SentenceTransformer with MiniLM or MPNet, which can produce a sentence or paragraph embedding efficiently on CPU. For example, all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is a 6-layer Transformer that is quite fast on CPU and can produce a 384-dimensional embedding for any text. We could feed the entire OCR text of the document (maybe truncated if extremely long) into such a model to get a single vector representation. This embedding will naturally reflect the words used: invoices will mention terms like “Invoice #, amount, due date,” leases will have “Lessee, Lessor, rent,” etc., which helps differentiate classes. If we decided to include layout tokens or tags in the text (like the bracketed coordinates), those would also go through the embedding model. However, a generic language model might not know what to make of numeric coordinates tokens – at worst they might add noise. If using something like LayoutLM (which has explicit layout processing), that would be ideal but it’s heavier. As a compromise, we could embed just the plain text content first.
Another option is to create custom features: for example, one can tokenize the document and also consider simple layout features such as the percentage of text in uppercase (maybe leases have a lot of all-caps headers), or count of occurrences of keywords (e.g. “Invoice” vs “Guaranty” vs “Lease”). Those could be fed into a classical classifier along with embeddings. But sticking to embeddings: using a pre-trained transformer to get a document vector is likely sufficient for clustering and initial classification.
	2.	Clustering Documents: Once each document (real or synthetic) is represented as a vector (say in ℝ^384), we can perform clustering using algorithms like K-Means or Hierarchical Agglomerative Clustering. K-Means with k=5 (for our 5 document types) is a simple choice if we assume we know there are 5 clusters. We should first reduce dimensionality for visualization and sanity check – e.g. use PCA or t-SNE on the embeddings to see if natural grouping occurs. We expect invoices to separate clearly from contracts, because their language and structure diverge a lot. The contracts (guarantee, loan, lease) might cluster closer since they all share legal language, but hopefully the model can pick up on differences (like “Guaranty” vs “Lease”). If not, we might incorporate bigram features or something to help. Since we also have synthetic labels, we can evaluate clustering purity by how well the clusters align with known types. If clustering shows confusion (e.g. guarantee vs loan not well separated), it could be that those need more distinct signals or more training data.
Clustering is unsupervised; if we have labels, a direct classification might be simpler. But clustering could help discover subgroups or anomalies (maybe our synthetic data forms one cluster and real data another – which would indicate domain gap we might need to fix by making synthetic more realistic).
	3.	Classification by Document Type: Ultimately, to automate document type recognition, training a classifier is ideal. We have a few approaches:
	•	Rule-based heuristics: as a quick solution, one can use the presence of certain keywords. For instance, if the word “Invoice” appears near the top and there’s a table of numbers, it’s likely an Invoice. If “Master Lease Agreement” appears, it’s a Lease. A guarantee might have “Guarantor” and be one page. These clues can be codified. However, rule-based approaches may be brittle (e.g. a lease might not have “Master” in title, or an invoice might not literally have the word “Invoice” if it’s a foreign language or some weird template).
	•	Supervised ML classifier: Using the embeddings above, we can train a lightweight classifier (logistic regression, SVM, or even a small neural network) to predict the document type. This would be very fast at runtime on CPU. For example, take 384-dim embeddings for a training set of docs, and train a logistic regression to classify among the 5 types. Since 5 classes is a manageable classification task, even a basic classifier could achieve high accuracy if the embeddings are good. We should of course use our synthetic documents to augment training, since real labeled examples might be few.
	•	Fine-tuning a model: We could also fine-tune a transformer (like LayoutLM or DistilBERT) on our labeled examples for sequence classification. This might improve accuracy by leveraging patterns in text and layout, but fine-tuning can be time-consuming and might require GPU to be efficient. If we restrict to CPU, a simpler model or a smaller set of features might be preferable.
Given that we aim for scalability on CPU, a pipeline might look like:
	1.	OCR the document to get text.
	2.	Feature extraction: compute embedding vector via a small transformer or even TF-IDF vector (for a classic approach).
	3.	Classification: apply a pre-trained classifier (like a saved logistic reg model) to output a document type label.
This should be quite fast per document (embedding with MiniLM might take a few hundred milliseconds on CPU, classification is negligible).
We should also incorporate the layout information if needed. For instance, one could append a couple of engineered features to the embedding, such as the aspect ratio of the document or number of pages (invoices are typically 1 page, loan/lease often multiple pages; guarantees often 1 page). Another feature: does the text contain a large table (we could detect if many numbers appear in columns – likely invoice). These simple cues combined with the semantic embedding would yield a very robust classifier.
Additionally, since the prompt mentions “optional LLM-based reasoning through a wrapper API”, we can integrate an LLM for cases where the automated classifier is unsure. For example, if a document is borderline between a loan vs lease agreement, we could send a subset of the text (perhaps the first paragraph or any detected title) to an LLM (like GPT-4 via API) with a prompt: “Determine if the following text is from a lease agreement, loan agreement, guarantee, invoice, or acceptance certificate: ‘…text…’. Respond with the document type.” The LLM’s answer can then be used if confident. This kind of reasoning might not be needed often if our classifier is good, but it’s a safety net for unusual documents. It’s also useful for extracting information beyond just classification – e.g. if later we want the system to answer questions about a contract, an LLM could be invoked. The wrapper could be set so that for 95% of documents the local system handles everything, but for the hardest 5% (or on user request), an LLM can be consulted, thereby balancing performance and accuracy with cost.
	4.	Clustering for Dataset Analysis: We mentioned clustering primarily as a way to group documents unsupervised. In practice, after building our embedding, we might cluster to see if any outliers or mistakes occur. For example, if a particular synthetic invoice looks very different and clusters away from other invoices, we may examine it and realize maybe its format was off. Clustering can also help in semi-supervised scenarios: if we have many unlabeled docs, we cluster them and then label one representative from each cluster to propagate labels. Since our scenario is mostly defined (we know the types), clustering is more of a diagnostic tool here.
	5.	Iterate and Refine: We should evaluate classification results on a validation set of documents. If certain confusions occur (say it confuses a guarantee vs a loan agreement), consider augmenting features. Possibly the guarantee being one page vs loans often multiple pages is a differentiator – we can include the page count as a feature. Or maybe include bigrams of important words (the word “Lessee” appears in leases, “Borrower” in loans, “Guarantor” in guarantees, “Invoice” in invoices, “Certificate” in delivery docs). These key terms could be part of a vocabulary that we specifically weight or use in a simpler classifier if needed. On CPU, something like a Naive Bayes on word presence might even do decently, but using modern embeddings plus a light classifier should achieve the goal with more flexibility.

In summary, tokenize and embed using a lightweight model (or even TF-IDF if extremely constrained, but semantic embeddings are better). Then either cluster to observe natural groupings or directly train a classifier. The output will be a model that given a new document’s OCR text (and possibly layout info), predicts the document type. This whole pipeline can run locally without heavy compute – especially if we avoid large models and use optimized libraries for embedding on CPU. By leveraging the distinct vocabulary and structural markers of each document type, the system can accurately classify them. And with the documents embedded in a vector space, we can also do similarity search – for example, retrieve similar documents to a query doc (helpful if one wanted to do a nearest-neighbor approach to classification or to cluster new incoming documents incrementally).
